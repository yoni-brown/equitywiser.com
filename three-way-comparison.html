Home Equity · The Three-Way Decision

HELOC vs. cash-out refi vs. home equity loan:
which one is actually yours?

Three products. Three homeowners with the same $60,000 need. Three different right answers — and the thing that decides among them is the one question most comparisons skip.

The short answer

All three products let you borrow against home equity. The one that fits you depends mostly on a single fact: the rate on the mortgage you already have. If your rate is below 5%, a HELOC or a home equity loan wins almost every time — because a cash-out refinance forces you to replace a low rate you can't easily get back. If your rate is above 6%, a cash-out refinance becomes genuinely competitive: it can lower the rate on your whole mortgage balance while funding what you need, in one transaction. Between the HELOC and the home equity loan, the split is about discipline and certainty — HELOC for phased or flexible needs, home equity loan for a single fixed payment on a known amount.

Most three-way comparisons run into the same problem: by the time you finish reading about all three products, you've been told twelve different things can be "the right choice," and the article has somehow committed to none of them. That's the lender's house style, and it isn't useful. The truth is narrower and more helpful. For any specific homeowner with a specific existing rate, a specific amount to borrow, and a specific time horizon, the answer among these three is usually obvious — once you know where to look.

Where to look is the rate on your current mortgage. New York Fed researchers put the frame plainly in 2024: by the end of 2023, nearly 70% of outstanding U.S. mortgages carried rates at least three percentage points below what a new mortgage would cost. For that 70%, a cash-out refinance is a losing trade — it replaces a rate you can't easily get back. For the remaining 30%, most of whom bought or refinanced in 2023 or later at 6%+, the math inverts: a cash-out refinance lowers the rate on their whole mortgage balance at the same time it delivers cash. The three-way decision isn't really a toss-up. It's a routing problem. Below: the three products side by side, the math for three borrowers who sort cleanly into three different answers, and the decision tree that gets you to yours.

The one question that decides among them

Read the "affects your first mortgage?" row in the table above again. That's the hinge. A HELOC and a home equity loan sit on top of whatever existing mortgage you have — they don't touch it. This is what lenders mean by a “second lien”: a loan that sits behind your first mortgage in line to be paid off if the house is ever sold or foreclosed. A cash-out refinance is different. It replaces your existing first mortgage with a brand-new, bigger one at whatever rate you can get today. Those are different products. They feel similar because they all deliver cash from home equity. They aren't.

The practical consequence is blunt. If the rate on your current mortgage is low — say, the 3%–4% that roughly 70% of American homeowners are still sitting on — a cash-out refinance charges you a rate-reset penalty on the entire existing balance, not just the cash you pulled. That penalty usually outweighs the rate advantage on the cash. A HELOC or home equity loan leaves the low rate alone. If the rate on your current mortgage is already high — 6%+ — the reset disappears, because today's cash-out refinance rate (~6.62%) is in the same neighborhood or lower. Now the cash-out refi starts doing two jobs at once, and it wins.

Between the HELOC and the home equity loan, the hinge is different: discipline and certainty. A HELOC gives you flexibility (draw as you need) but requires you to pay principal down voluntarily during the draw period to avoid a payment jump at year 10. A home equity loan gives you a fixed monthly payment from day one, no flexibility, no surprises. For borrowers who know exactly how much they need and want payment predictability, the home equity loan is the right tool. For borrowers with phased needs who are disciplined about paying down what they draw, the HELOC is cheaper. For everyone else, the home equity loan is the lower-risk default.

The math, for three homeowners with the same need

Three homeowners each need $60,000 in cash. They are otherwise different in exactly the ways that matter for this decision. The math splits them into three different winners.

Scenario

$60,000 needed — three borrowers, three winners

Tom — locked in at 3.25% (2021 refi), needs $60K for a phased renovation over several years

  • Current mortgage: $260,000 balance at 3.25%, $1,132/mo payment
  • HELOC at 7.14%, disciplined principal + interest over 10 years: $701/mo on top of mortgage = $1,833/mo combined. HELOC paid off at year 10. Total HELOC interest: $24,119.
  • Home equity loan at 7.47% fixed, 15-yr: $555/mo on top of mortgage = $1,687/mo combined. Predictable but slower payoff; balance of ~$27,700 remains at year 10.
  • Cash-out refi to $320,000 at 6.62%: $2,048/mo single payment — but the rate-reset penalty on the existing $260K adds roughly $98,000 in extra interest over 10 years vs. the HELOC path.
  • Winner: HELOC (or home equity loan if he wants payment certainty at slightly higher cost). The low first-mortgage rate is worth preserving.

Lisa — bought in 2023 at 7.25%, needs $60K to consolidate credit card debt at 22% APR

  • Current mortgage: $260,000 balance at 7.25%, $1,774/mo payment
  • HELOC interest-only at 7.14%: $357/mo on top of mortgage = $2,131/mo combined. Variable rate risk. Still owes $60K at year 10.
  • Home equity loan at 7.47% fixed, 15-yr: $555/mo on top of mortgage = $2,329/mo combined. Most expensive path; pays down slowly.
  • Cash-out refi to $320,000 at 6.62%: $2,048/mo single payment, $83/mo less than the HELOC path and ~$22,000 less in interest over 10 years. 6.62% is below her existing 7.25%, so the rate reset is a rate improvement.
  • Winner: cash-out refinance. Lower rate on the whole balance, one payment, kills the 22% APR on the cards.

Ray — age 61, 5.25% mortgage, needs $60K for aging-in-place modifications before retirement

  • Current mortgage: $200,000 balance at 5.25%, $1,104/mo payment. Retirement in 3 years; fixed income coming.
  • HELOC at 7.14%, disciplined principal + interest over 10 years: $701/mo, $24,119 total interest. Cheapest on paper — but a 2-percentage-point rate rise pushes the payment past $800 and adds tens of thousands to the total. Ray's post-retirement income can't absorb that.
  • Home equity loan at 7.47% fixed, 10 years: $711/mo, $25,353 total interest, paid off at age 71. Fixed rate, fixed payment, predictable.
  • Cash-out refi: gives up 5.25% for 6.62% — the rate-reset penalty adds ~$35,000 over 10 years, before counting closing costs. Not competitive.
  • Winner: home equity loan. The ~$10/mo cost over the HELOC is the price of locking in certainty for a fixed-income future.

All figures computed against standard amortization formulas at April 2026 rates; HELOC rate 7.14%, home equity loan rate 7.47%, cash-out refi rate 6.62% for a 720+ credit score at 80% combined loan-to-value. Combined loan-to-value is the total of your first mortgage plus any home-equity borrowing divided by your home's value.

Three identical cash needs. Three different winning products. None of the three answers is the result of one product being "better" — it's the result of each product being the right fit for one specific starting situation. Tom's low first-mortgage rate makes the cash-out refi prohibitively expensive and the HELOC the obvious winner. Lisa's high first-mortgage rate flips that: the cash-out refi saves her money in two ways at once, and the HELOC's variable rate is just another uncertainty she doesn't need. Ray's approaching retirement makes the payment certainty of a home equity loan worth the small premium over a HELOC that could move against him at the worst time.

All three products are the right answer for someone. The question is whether someone is you.

The feature differences that actually matter

A feature-by-feature comparison can bury the borrower in trivia. The four that actually change the decision:

  • Does it touch your first mortgage? Only the cash-out refinance does. This is the single biggest mechanical difference. It's why a low existing rate changes which product fits.
  • Is the rate variable or fixed? HELOC is variable — it tracks the U.S. Prime Rate (the benchmark most consumer loans use, currently 6.75%) and moves when the Fed moves. Home equity loan and cash-out refinance are fixed at closing. If you can't stomach payment changes, the HELOC is the wrong product at any existing rate.
  • Is the money delivered as a lump sum or as you draw? HELOC draws are flexible; home equity loan and cash-out refi are lump sums at closing. For phased needs (a multi-year renovation, tuition over years), the HELOC's flexibility is worth real money in interest saved. For one-shot needs, it isn't.
  • Can the lender freeze the funds? HELOCs can be suspended under federal rules if home values fall meaningfully or your financial condition deteriorates. Home equity loan proceeds and cash-out refi proceeds are already in your account — there's nothing to freeze. In a soft housing market, that distinction stops being theoretical.

Closing costs feel like another big difference — and they matter — but they're essentially a scaling factor. HELOCs often run $0–$2,000 and sometimes waive everything. Home equity loans and cash-out refinances both charge 2%–5% of the loan amount. For a cash-out refinance, that percentage is applied to the full new loan, not just the cash-out portion: on a $320,000 refi to access $60,000 in cash, 3% closing costs are $9,600 — you're paying origination costs to refinance money you'd already borrowed.

Four things borrowers miss

1. The rate-reset penalty is usually the biggest number in the decision

For a borrower replacing a low-rate mortgage with a cash-out refi, most of the increased monthly payment has nothing to do with the cash they pulled out. In Tom's math above, of the $916/mo jump from his current mortgage to the cash-out refi, roughly 70% is re-pricing the existing $260,000 from 3.25% to 6.62% — the cash itself accounts for only about 30%. That split is invisible on most cash-out calculators, which show the full new payment without separating it into "old money at a new rate" and "new money." Always separate the two. You're much more likely to stomach the "new money" cost than to agree to the "old money at new rate" cost once you see them apart.

2. HELOC and home equity loan rates are nearly identical right now

Historically HELOCs were meaningfully cheaper than home equity loans because lenders priced in the borrower's variable-rate risk. That gap has mostly closed at April 2026 rates: HELOC around 7.02% variable, home equity loan around 7.47% fixed — roughly 0.45 percentage points apart. On a $60,000 balance, that's about $22/month. For many borrowers, saving $22/month isn't worth carrying variable-rate risk for 10–20 years. The calculus that made HELOCs the automatic winner a decade ago doesn't hold in 2026.

3. Closing costs on a cash-out refi run on the full new loan

The single most misread number in a three-way comparison. On a $60,000 cash need that results in a $320,000 cash-out refi, the 2%–5% closing cost applies to the full $320,000 — $6,400 to $16,000. A HELOC covering the same $60,000 might cost $500 to $1,500 in closing costs. A home equity loan for $60,000 typically costs $1,800 to $3,000. Scale matters: the cash-out refi's rate advantage has to overcome a 3–10× larger closing cost bill before it wins.

4. Your lender's default recommendation may not be your best option

A 2023 Center for Responsible Lending study flagged that FHA (Federal Housing Administration) cash-out refinances disproportionately affect borrowers with lower credit scores, residents of neighborhoods with higher shares of Black residents, and low-wealth homeowners — exactly the borrowers whose math often points toward a HELOC or home equity loan instead. The loan officer's incentives are not aligned with yours: cash-out refinances generate larger origination fees than HELOCs or home equity loans do. If the product being pitched to you is the one that earns the most for the person pitching it, that's a signal to run the numbers yourself, not skip them.

The decision tree

Strip the marketing, and the three-way decision reduces to a short sequence of questions. Run yours through it.

Start here: your existing mortgage rate

  • Below ~5%: Cash-out refinance is almost always wrong. The rate-reset penalty on your existing balance will outweigh anything. Move to HELOC vs. home equity loan.
  • 5%–6%: Cash-out refinance is competitive only if you need a large amount ($100K+) with a long horizon (7+ years). Otherwise, prefer a second lien.
  • Above 6%: Cash-out refinance is genuinely in play. Compare the cash-out rate to your existing rate — if it's lower or within a fraction of a point, the refi often wins for larger amounts.
  • No existing mortgage (paid off): Cash-out refi isn't really the right framing. Compare HELOC and home equity loan, and weigh a reverse mortgage if you're 62 or older.

Next: HELOC vs. home equity loan

  • Phased or uncertain need (multi-year renovation, tuition over years, ongoing medical): HELOC. You only pay interest on what you draw.
  • Known one-time amount, want payment certainty: home equity loan. Fixed rate, fixed payment, no variable-rate risk.
  • Approaching retirement or on fixed income: home equity loan. The HELOC's ~$22/month savings on a $60K balance isn't worth the variable-rate exposure.
  • Local housing market is falling: home equity loan. A HELOC line can be frozen; a home equity loan's funds are already disbursed.
  • Disciplined enough to pay principal down during the draw period: HELOC. The disciplined path is the cheapest option among all three products.

When none of the three fits

Sometimes the honest answer is that no home-equity product fits the situation. A few cases where that's true:

  • You need less than ~$15,000. Any home-equity product's closing costs, annual fees, or rate premium over the alternative eats the benefit at small amounts. A personal loan (fast, unsecured, fixed payment) or a 0%-intro balance-transfer credit card, for the disciplined, usually beats all three. We have a dedicated comparison of HELOC vs. personal loan for this case.
  • The money is going to fund ongoing spending. Vacations, cars, wedding expenses. None of the three products should pay for things that don't build lasting value — the interest compounds for decades and the memory doesn't.
  • You're consolidating credit card debt driven by a spending pattern, not a one-time event. Converting unsecured debt into a home-secured product lowers the rate and raises the stakes. Navy Federal Credit Union's own consumer-education materials are blunt on this: consolidation "addresses the symptom but not the problem if overspending caused the debt in the first place." The common failure mode — paying off the cards and running them back up within 18 months — now has the house on the line. Fix the spending first.
  • Your income is unstable. All three products turn your home into collateral for a monthly payment you have to make every month, regardless of what happens. If the answer to "can I afford this payment for the next 10 years in a bad scenario?" isn't a clear yes, the answer to "which product?" is none of them.
  • You're 62 or older and cash-flow-strapped. A reverse mortgage (HECM) is a separate product category that can work when none of the three here fits — specifically for retirees who can't carry a monthly payment at all. It has its own costs (2–4% upfront, a loan balance that grows until you move or pass) and risks (property-tax default is the leading cause of HECM foreclosure). See our dedicated guide.

The decision, in one paragraph

All three products deliver cash from home equity, and all three put the home on the line. That's where the similarity ends. A cash-out refinance is the right answer when your existing mortgage rate is already above the current refinance rate — it does two jobs at once and doesn't cost you a rate you can't get back. A home equity loan is the right answer when you know exactly what you need, want a fixed payment for a set number of years, and value certainty over flexibility — especially if you're approaching retirement or your local market is soft. A HELOC is the right answer when your needs are phased or flexible, you're disciplined about paying down what you draw, and you want to preserve a low first-mortgage rate you can't replicate today. The honest three-way comparison isn't a toss-up. For any specific homeowner at any specific moment, the answer is usually obvious once the starting situation is known. Run your numbers. The product that fits will announce itself.
