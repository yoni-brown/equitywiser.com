HELOC vs. 401(k) loan:
the hidden costs of each.

The rate on a 401(k) loan is cheaper. The true cost isn't. Here's why — and when borrowing from your retirement account actually makes sense.

The short answer

A 401(k) loan carries a lower stated rate than a HELOC — typically Prime + 1% (about 7.75% today) vs. a HELOC's 7.02% national average — and the interest goes back into your own account. That sounds unbeatable. It isn't. The interest you pay yourself replaces the market returns your 401(k) would otherwise earn, which over decades averages about 10%. On a $40,000 loan over 5 years, the missed growth adds roughly $15,000–$25,000 to the true cost. And if you leave your job with the loan outstanding, a 10% penalty plus income tax can take close to 40 cents on every borrowed dollar. A HELOC costs more on paper and usually less in practice.

The 401(k) loan is sold on a line that sounds unbeatable: why pay interest to a bank when you can pay it to yourself? It's a tidy sentence. It's also the wrong way to think about the decision. A 401(k) loan is not a clever trick that turns a lender's profit into your own. It's a swap — your investment growth for a lower interest rate on the loan — and the swap usually runs against you. Over any meaningful time horizon, the money you borrow out of a retirement account isn't earning what it would have earned if you'd left it alone, and the difference is rarely made up by the interest you pay to yourself along the way.

A HELOC, meanwhile, has problems of its own: it's variable-rate, secured by your house, and vulnerable to being frozen when home values fall. What it does not do is tax your retirement wealth. For borrowers with real home equity and a job they expect to keep, the HELOC almost always wins the math once the forgone investment growth is priced in. The exceptions exist and are worth naming. This page lays out both products, runs the true-cost math on a $40,000 borrowing need, and tells you when each one is the right tool — not just the cheaper-looking one.

The “pay yourself interest” myth

The selling point of a 401(k) loan is that the interest payment is a transfer from one pocket to another: your paycheck to your retirement account. It feels like you're beating the bank. You're not. The money you borrow is money that's no longer invested, and the interest you pay yourself is not a bonus — it's a substitute for the market returns you gave up.

Michael Kitces, a widely respected independent financial planner who writes at Kitces.com, frames it plainly: “Paying 401(k) loan interest to yourself is really just contributing your own money to your own 401(k) account, without any growth at all.” The elevator version: when you borrow $40,000 out of your 401(k), that $40,000 is no longer in the market. The S&P 500 has averaged about 10% annual returns over the last century, including dividends reinvested. A 7.75% interest payment back to yourself replaces those returns. It's a lower number on the same account.

Put another way: the day before you borrow, your retirement dollars are earning 10% on average. The day after, they're earning 7.75% (because the borrowed portion is the loan you're paying to yourself, not stock). The gap — about 2.25 percentage points, compounded — is the hidden cost. It doesn't show up on any loan statement. It shows up in your retirement balance decades later.

There's a second layer. The interest you pay yourself comes out of your paycheck, which is after-tax money. When you eventually withdraw that money in retirement, the interest portion is taxed again as ordinary income. On a $40,000 loan at 7.75% over 5 years, that's about $8,300 in interest — and the double taxation on that interest, assuming a 22% federal tax bracket in retirement, costs you roughly $1,870 extra. It's not the main story. But it is a story.

The math, including the hidden cost

Two homeowners need $40,000 for a renovation over 5 years. Alex borrows from his 401(k). Dana takes a HELOC. On the loan statement, Alex looks ahead. At retirement, he's behind.

Scenario

$40,000 borrowed over 5 years — 401(k) loan vs. HELOC

Alex — $40,000 401(k) loan at 7.75% for 5 years

  • Monthly payment (payroll-deducted): $805
  • Total interest paid (to his own 401(k)): ~$8,300
  • Opportunity cost — forgone market returns on the $40K while it's out of the account: ~$15,000–$25,000 at 10% average returns
  • Double taxation on the $8,300 of interest (22% bracket, at retirement): ~$1,870
  • Possible lost employer match if loan repayments crowd out regular contributions: $2,000–$10,000+
  • True net cost: ~$18,000–$35,000 in retirement wealth not accumulated

Dana — $40,000 HELOC at 7.02% paid down over 5 years

  • Monthly payment (principal + interest): $792
  • Total interest paid (to the lender): ~$7,520
  • Opportunity cost on her 401(k): $0 — the retirement account stays fully invested
  • Closing costs: $0–$2,000 (many lenders waive for lines under $50K)
  • Tax deduction (if funds are used for substantial home improvement and Dana itemizes at 22%): ~$1,650 saved
  • True net cost: ~$5,900–$9,500 in out-of-pocket interest

Opportunity cost assumes Alex redirects normal retirement contributions to loan repayments, which most borrowers do. The 10% historical return assumption comes from roughly a century of S&P 500 data (including dividends reinvested); a specific five-year window can be much higher or lower. HELOC closing costs are shown smaller than the usual 2–5% range because HELOC lines under $50,000 are often offered fee-free at credit unions and regional banks.

Read the two rows carefully. On the loan statement — the thing you look at during the decision — Alex's 401(k) loan costs about $800 less in interest than Dana's HELOC over five years. That difference is real, and it's the number loan officers, finance blogs, and general-interest articles tend to stop at. But Alex's retirement account has lost roughly $15,000 to $25,000 of growth it would have earned if left alone, and that gap never closes. Dana's retirement account is untouched. In a full comparison, the HELOC is not just cheaper on the wealth ledger — it's cheaper by a multiple.

The job-loss tax bomb

The second large hidden cost isn't about compounding — it's about what happens if you leave the job while the loan is still outstanding. Under current rules, you have until the following year's tax deadline to repay the full balance. If you can't, the IRS treats the outstanding amount as a distribution from your 401(k), and for a borrower under 59½, that means federal income tax plus a 10% early-withdrawal penalty plus state income tax where applicable.

The Profit Sharing/401(k) Council of America estimates that, on a typical default by a participant under 59½, roughly 40 cents of every borrowed dollar ends up going to the government — 25% federal tax, 5% state tax, 10% penalty. On a $30,000 outstanding balance, that's about $12,000 in combined tax and penalty, plus the permanent loss of the $30,000 from retirement savings, plus every decade of compounding growth that $30,000 would have produced.

This isn't a theoretical risk. Job separation is the single most common cause of 401(k) loan defaults. The median tenure of an American worker at one employer is under five years — shorter than the standard five-year repayment term on a 401(k) loan. If your working life looks like the national average, there's meaningful probability that you'll leave the job before the loan is paid off, whether by choice or not. The mechanics don't care how it happened. A typical question on personal-finance forums captures the moment clearly: a borrower with an $11,000 loan outstanding, suddenly between jobs, trying to decide whether to take out another loan (credit card, personal loan, cash from savings) just to repay the 401(k) loan before the default deadline — or simply accept the tax bomb and move on.

A HELOC has no equivalent. If you lose your job with a HELOC outstanding, the loan terms don't change. You still owe the money, and losing a HELOC to foreclosure is a possible outcome if you stop paying for months on end — but there's no 10% penalty, no immediate tax bill, no sudden deadline forcing you to come up with $30,000 in cash. The worst-case HELOC outcome takes years to materialize. The worst-case 401(k)-loan outcome is triggered by a tax filing deadline.

Four things borrowers get wrong

1. The interest rate is almost irrelevant

The gap between a 7.02% HELOC and a 7.75% 401(k) loan is 0.75 percentage points — roughly $150 a year in interest on a $40,000 balance. The opportunity cost on the same $40,000 at 10% historical returns is several thousand dollars a year. Which of those numbers is the one that should drive the decision? The answer isn't the one in 72-point type on the comparison chart.

2. Most borrowers stop contributing during repayment

401(k) loan repayments come out of the same paycheck that was funding retirement contributions. In practice, many borrowers reduce or suspend contributions during the repayment period to absorb the loan payment. If your employer matches the first 6% of salary and you stop contributing, you've forfeited that match for every year the loan is outstanding. On a $100,000 salary with a 3% match (50% on the first 6%), that's $3,000 a year — another $15,000 over a 5-year loan you may not have counted.

3. A 401(k) loan default permanently reduces your retirement

If the loan defaults (most commonly at job separation), you can't repay it back into the 401(k) after the grace period. You can't roll the amount back in. You can't undo the taxable distribution. The money is gone from your retirement account, and the taxes and penalties are gone from your bank account. The effect compounds over decades: at 30 years old, a $30,000 loan default doesn't just cost you $30,000 and $12,000 in taxes today — it costs you the roughly $500,000 to $800,000 that $30,000 would have grown into by age 65 at long-run market returns of 8%–10%.

4. “No credit check” is genuinely an advantage — for some borrowers

Not all advantages of a 401(k) loan are myths. One is real: you don't need a credit score to qualify. If your credit has been damaged by medical debt, a recent divorce, or a bankruptcy, a HELOC may simply be unavailable — or available only at punishing rates. A 401(k) loan bypasses that entirely. It's also faster to fund than a HELOC (1–2 weeks vs. 2–6) and has no collateral on your home. For credit-damaged borrowers with genuine short-term needs, the 401(k) loan is sometimes the only reasonable option. This article argues it's usually not the better one. It never argues it's always wrong.

Which product wins, and for whom

Two conditions have to line up before a 401(k) loan is the right answer: you can't get a HELOC or the HELOC numbers are genuinely worse, and the need is urgent enough that the opportunity cost is outweighed by something else. Both conditions are rare.

Choose a HELOC when

  • You have meaningful home equity (15–20%+) and reasonable credit.
  • Your job situation is stable enough that a HELOC's 10-to-20-year payoff window isn't terrifying.
  • You want your 401(k) to keep compounding — which, for borrowers under 50, is almost always the right call.
  • The funds are for home improvements and you itemize deductions (HELOC interest can be deductible; 401(k) loan interest can't).
  • You can handle a variable rate and understand the freeze risk if home values fall materially.

Choose a 401(k) loan when

  • You can't qualify for a HELOC at a reasonable rate (thin equity, damaged credit, underwater market).
  • Your job is unusually stable (long-tenured union job, government, pension-eligible), making default risk small.
  • The need is genuinely short-term (under 12 months), so the opportunity cost is small in absolute dollars.
  • You're close to retirement and the compounding horizon that makes the opportunity cost damaging is already short.
  • The only realistic alternative is a predatory rate (payday, high-APR credit card) and a 401(k) loan is the lesser evil.

There's a third case worth naming. If you have real home equity and stable income, the honest answer is: don't do either, if you can avoid it. Both products involve trading something durable for a temporary need, and the math below rarely makes either look like a winning move in absolute terms — only relative to each other.

When neither is right

A borrowing decision is already losing ground if one of these applies. The right move is to fix the underlying problem, not to choose between two ways of financing it.

  • The purpose is discretionary. A vacation, a wedding, a car upgrade. Neither your house nor your retirement account should fund a two-week trip. The interest compounds for decades; the memory doesn't.
  • You're already carrying substantial debt. Adding a HELOC or 401(k) loan on top of existing credit card balances, student loans, or auto loans doesn't fix the debt; it rearranges the furniture. Until the underlying spending or income issue is addressed, every new loan is a ticking clock.
  • Your income is unstable. Both products assume you can make the payment every month until the loan is retired. The 401(k) loan adds the job-loss tax-bomb risk on top of that. If the answer to “can I make this payment for five years?” isn't a confident yes, the answer to “which loan?” is neither.
  • You could use a 0% balance-transfer card or personal savings. For amounts under ~$15,000 and a clear 12–18-month payoff, a promotional balance-transfer offer or a genuine emergency fund beats either structured loan on every dimension.
  • You're within a few years of retirement. A HELOC's variable rate paired with a fixed retirement income is dangerous, and a 401(k) loan shortens an already-short compounding horizon. Consider a fixed-rate home equity loan instead — or simply not borrowing.

The decision, in one paragraph

The 401(k) loan is the product that looks clever on the brochure and usually isn't. The interest rate is a fraction of a point below a HELOC's, but the money you borrow isn't earning the 10% your retirement account would otherwise average — and over five years on a $40,000 loan, that gap is $15,000 to $25,000 of retirement wealth that doesn't come back. Add the job-loss tax bomb (40% of the balance to the government if you default before 59½) and the 401(k) loan is the expensive choice for almost anyone with a job and some home equity. The exceptions are real: damaged credit that disqualifies you from a HELOC, a very short-term need, an unusually stable job situation. Outside those narrow cases, the HELOC is almost always the cheaper loan. Not on the statement — in the bank account that matters most.
