HELOC vs. personal loan:
which one is actually better for what you need?

A HELOC is cheaper money. A personal loan is faster, simpler, and doesn't put your house on the line. The right answer is whichever wins the math and the life test — and those split by size and time horizon.

The short answer

A HELOC is cheaper money — about 7.02% on average at April 2026 rates, versus around 12% for a typical personal loan. A personal loan is simpler and faster — funded in one to five days, no home collateral, one fixed payment for a set number of months. For small or short-term needs (under about $20,000, or paid off within two to three years), the personal loan's lower origination cost and faster funding often match or beat the HELOC despite the higher rate. For larger or longer-term needs, the HELOC's rate advantage compounds past any closing cost. The product isn't the decision. The loan size and time horizon are.

Most comparisons between HELOCs and personal loans stop at the rate. HELOC wins — it's cheaper. Story over. That comparison is right about the rate and wrong about almost everything else. A personal loan has no closing costs in the HELOC sense, funds in days instead of weeks, doesn't put your house on the line, and comes with one fixed payment for a set number of months — no variable-rate exposure, no payment jump at year ten, no frozen line when your neighborhood's values drop. The personal loan is a different product, not a worse one.

What settles the decision is the math-after-everything. The rate gap between a 7% HELOC and a 12% personal loan is five percentage points, which sounds decisive until you put it next to the $500 to $1,500 in HELOC closing costs, the $200 to $500 in potential early-termination fees, the two-to-six-week funding timeline, and the simple fact that your home is the collateral. At the right size and the right term, the HELOC is the obvious answer. At the wrong size or the wrong term, it isn't. Below: the rate picture, the math for two borrowers with very different needs, and the specific tests to run before you sign.

The rate picture at April 2026

HELOC rates are at a three-and-a-half-year low. Personal loan rates are close to their ten-year high. That's the headline and it's true — but the headline masks two things that change which product actually fits you.

First, personal loan rates vary dramatically by credit score (the FICO score most lenders use). The "12%" figure cited above is for borrowers with around a 700 score. Excellent-credit borrowers (740+) can get personal loans in the 6%–8% range — close to HELOC territory and sometimes beating it once you factor in HELOC closing costs. Fair-credit borrowers (630–689) routinely pay 16%–26%. Poor-credit borrowers pay 26%–36%. There is no "average" personal loan rate that applies to you; there is only the rate your specific credit profile earns.

Second, the unsecured personal-loan market is enormous and growing. TransUnion reported a record 7.2 million unsecured personal loan originations in the third quarter of 2025, with total balances reaching $276 billion by year-end — also a record. Digital lenders (SoFi, LendingClub, Upstart, Upgrade, and similar) originated $47 billion in personal loans in 2025 alone, up 23% year over year, and now account for more than 58% of all unsecured personal loan originations. This is why a personal loan can actually fund in 1–5 days now: the infrastructure genuinely exists at scale, in a way it didn't five years ago.

The math, for two borrowers

Abstractions don't fund projects. Here's the decision worked out for two homeowners who need different amounts of money for different time horizons.

Scenario

Same products, different needs, opposite answers

Ana — needs $15,000 for a repair, wants it paid off in 3 years

  • Personal loan at 12.0% for 36 months: $498/mo, $17,936 total, funded in 3–5 days
  • HELOC at 7.14% paid off in 36 months: $464/mo, $16,707 + $1,000 closing costs = $17,707 total, funded in 2–4 weeks
  • HELOC advantage: ~$229. A $500 early-termination fee (common on HELOCs closed within 2–3 years) flips the math back: personal loan wins by about $270
  • Tiebreaker: the personal loan funded in days and didn't put the house up. That matters more than $229 over three years.

Ben — needs $75,000 for a major renovation, wants a 10-year payoff

  • Personal loan at 11% for 7 years (the typical lender cap): $1,285/mo, $107,900 total. No 10-year option at most lenders.
  • HELOC at 7.14% paid off over 10 years: $876/mo, $105,168 + $1,500 closing costs = $106,668 total
  • HELOC advantage: ~$408/month lower payment across 7 years, ~$1,200 total saved, and the 10-year term the personal loan can't offer
  • If Ben makes PL-sized payments on the HELOC voluntarily, the line is paid off in ~6 years and he saves roughly $14,000 in interest

All figures assume a 700 credit score for the personal loan rate and a 720 for the HELOC rate. Personal loan rate capped at 7-year term because most major lenders (SoFi, Discover, LightStream, Upgrade) don't offer terms longer than 84 months.

Read the two rows side by side. Ana's decision is close enough that the non-rate factors (speed, collateral, simplicity) swing the answer. Ben's decision isn't close — the combination of a lower rate and access to a longer term is worth several hundred dollars a month, every month, for as long as the loan runs.

Under about $20,000, the personal loan is often the smarter answer even though it's the more expensive one. Past about $50,000, it usually isn't.

The collateral trade

The single biggest difference between a HELOC and a personal loan is also the one that's easiest to leave out of the comparison spreadsheet: if you default, a HELOC can cost you the house. A personal loan cannot.

Personal loans are unsecured. If you miss payments, the lender's remedies are the ones every unsecured creditor has: report the delinquency to the credit bureaus, send the debt to collections, possibly sue for a judgment and garnish wages in states that allow it. None of those outcomes are small, and a personal loan default can do meaningful damage to your credit for seven years. But your house stays yours.

A HELOC is a mortgage. Missing payments eventually leads to foreclosure. The timeline is long — a lender will usually work with you for months before filing — but the ultimate remedy exists, and it exists for a reason: the lender's loss in a default is limited by the collateral, which means the lender will protect that collateral by any legal means necessary. That's what "secured" means.

In normal times, when employment is stable and home values are rising, that distinction feels theoretical. It isn't. The gap between "bruised credit" and "selling the house under pressure" is the gap between these two products, and it gets more important in exactly the economic conditions that make borrowing more likely in the first place — recessions, income shocks, medical events. A borrower whose job situation feels uncertain should add a visible risk premium to the HELOC side of the calculation. That premium doesn't show up on the rate sheet.

Four things borrowers get wrong

1. Personal loan rates vary more than any other consumer product

The "average 12%" number hides an enormous spread. A borrower with a 620 credit score might pay 28% on a personal loan when the same borrower pays 10% on a HELOC — an 18-percentage-point gap. A borrower with a 770 credit score might pay 6.5% on a personal loan when the same borrower pays 7.0% on a HELOC — a half-point gap that reverses the usual order. Never compare "HELOC vs. personal loan" in the abstract. Compare the specific quote each lender would actually give you at your specific score.

2. "No closing cost" HELOCs have early-termination fees

Many HELOC lenders advertise zero closing costs. Read the disclosure. It's almost always paired with an early-termination fee that claws the waived costs back if you close the account within 24–36 months. Bank of America charges $450 within 36 months. U.S. Bank charges 1% of the original line (up to $500) within 30 months. Truist can claw back up to $10,000. If there's any chance you'll refinance, move, or pay the HELOC off early, price that fee into the comparison. The $0 closing cost isn't a gift; it's a loan.

3. Origination fees on personal loans can rewrite the rate

Some personal lenders advertise low rates but charge an origination fee of 1% to 10% of the loan, deducted from the funded amount or added to the principal. A 10% origination fee on a $15,000 loan is $1,500 — roughly the same as a HELOC's closing costs, but wired directly into the effective interest rate. Always compare the annual percentage rate (APR), not the headline rate. The APR bakes in origination fees; the rate alone doesn't. A 10% rate with a 6% origination fee can have a higher APR than a 13% no-fee loan.

4. HELOCs and personal loans hit your credit differently

A HELOC is reported as revolving credit (like a credit card). A personal loan is reported as installment credit (like a car loan). The FICO scoring model — the credit score most lenders use — treats revolving-credit balances as part of your credit utilization ratio, so a maxed-out HELOC can tank your score. Installment loans don't affect utilization at all. Newer FICO versions specifically exclude HELOCs from utilization, but VantageScore (the other major scoring model) still includes them. The upshot: borrowers planning a major purchase in the next 12 months (another mortgage, a car loan) should weigh the score impact carefully. A personal loan is generally more score-neutral, drawn or not.

Which product wins, and for whom

Strip away the marketing, and the decision reduces to a handful of tests.

Choose a HELOC when

  • You need more than about $25,000–$30,000, where the rate advantage outruns closing costs.
  • Your payoff horizon is five or more years — and you want the option of a longer term than a personal loan allows.
  • Your expenses are phased over time (multi-year renovation, tuition, ongoing medical) and the draw flexibility is worth using.
  • You have strong credit and meaningful equity (20%+), and the combined loan-to-value math actually works.
  • You're disciplined about paying down what you draw, and you understand the variable-rate and freeze-risk exposure.

Choose a personal loan when

  • You need less than about $20,000 and the HELOC's closing costs eat the rate advantage.
  • You need the money in days, not weeks — an emergency repair, a time-sensitive purchase, a closing date.
  • You're a renter, a recent homebuyer with thin equity, or a homeowner in a soft market where you can't qualify for a HELOC.
  • Your payoff window is under three years, which is too short to pay back HELOC closing costs and squarely inside the early-termination-fee window.
  • You want certainty above all else — fixed rate, fixed term, fixed payment, no collateral on the house, no surprises at year ten.

Andrew Latham, content director at SuperMoney and a certified financial planner, framed the HELOC case directly for CBS News: the product “is optimal for homeowners who need to borrow gradually, like during a phased renovation, or want the flexibility of drawing funds only when needed.” That is the right frame. The HELOC shines on bigger, longer, flexible needs. The personal loan shines on smaller, faster, one-shot needs. Neither is better. They solve different problems.

When neither is right

Sometimes the honest answer is that borrowing isn't the right tool at all, or that a different tool entirely fits better.

  • You're consolidating credit card debt driven by a spending pattern, not a one-time event. Converting unsecured debt to secured debt via a HELOC lowers the rate and raises the stakes. A HELOC-and-re-run-the-cards outcome is the single most common regret on personal-finance forums, and it puts the house up as collateral for what was originally impulse spending. A personal loan at least keeps the debt unsecured, but it doesn't fix the underlying habit either. Until the spending is addressed, any borrowing is a ticking clock.
  • You're borrowing to chase a market. Pulling $50,000 out of home equity at 7% to invest at a hoped-for 10% return is leverage against your house. The math works on paper and fails in reality more often than it succeeds — because the rate you're paying is guaranteed, and the return you're chasing isn't. This is one of the rare cases where the consensus across independent personal-finance communities is nearly unanimous, and the consensus is against.
  • You're within a few years of retirement. A fixed, reduced income pairs poorly with either a variable-rate HELOC or a payment that carries well past your last paycheck. If you must borrow against the house at this stage, a fixed-rate home equity loan is usually the better choice — same collateral as a HELOC, but a fixed rate and fixed payment.
  • The amount is too small to justify either. For amounts under ~$5,000 and a 12-month payoff, a 0% balance-transfer credit card (for the disciplined) or simply paying cash over time usually beats both products. Closing costs and origination fees don't earn themselves back over tiny balances.
  • Your income is genuinely unstable. Either product turns a financial shock into a compounding emergency. When the answer to "can you afford this payment every month for three years?" isn't a clear yes, the answer to "which loan?" is neither.

The decision, in one paragraph

A HELOC is the cheaper money for larger, longer-term borrowing. A personal loan is the faster, simpler, safer money for smaller, shorter-term borrowing. The threshold that separates them depends on your specific rate quotes, but a useful rule of thumb is this: under about $20,000 with a two-to-three-year payoff, the personal loan usually wins the all-in math once you factor closing costs, speed, and collateral. Over about $50,000 with a five-plus-year payoff, the HELOC wins by several hundred dollars a month. In the middle, check the specific quotes against the specific timeline — and weigh the non-math factors honestly. Putting your home up to save a few hundred dollars over three years isn't a trade most people, on reflection, actually want to make. Neither loan is better. Match the product to the job.
