HELOC vs. Home Equity Loan:
What's actually different?

Two products, same collateral, very different risks. Here's what the math looks like at April 2026 rates — and why the answer isn't always the one your lender is selling.

The short answer

A HELOC is a revolving credit line tied to the Prime Rate — you draw what you need, pay interest only on what you've drawn, and the rate moves when the Fed does. A home equity loan is a fixed lump sum at a fixed rate, paid out at closing. Both are secured by your house. For most of the past decade, the rule was simple: HELOCs cost more than home equity loans because the variable rate was riskier for the lender. That flipped. As of April 2026, HELOC rates (~7.02%) are actually lower than home equity loan rates (~6.95%–8.05%) — thanks to the Fed's 2024–2025 rate cuts. But the lower rate only turns into lower total cost if you pay down principal from day one. The real question isn't rate. It's whether your expenses truly spread across years, how much you could afford if rates climb, and whether you trust yourself with a revolving line.

Both products do the same basic thing: they let you borrow against the equity you've built in your home. At first glance, they look interchangeable — same collateral, same general purpose, similar rates. But the mechanics diverge in ways that can cost or save you thousands of dollars over a decade, and the differences mostly surface in edge cases: a rate change, a move, a job loss, a soft housing market. Which is exactly when they start to matter.

Most of what lenders say flattens the decision into a rule: “HELOCs are flexible, home equity loans are predictable.” True, but not useful. The real question is which specific borrower, with which specific need, in which specific rate environment, comes out ahead — and what they lose in the trade. Below: the math, and the fine print your lender isn't advertising.

The mechanics, side by side

A home equity loan is the straightforward one. You borrow a fixed amount — say, $60,000 — at a fixed rate, and you get the full amount as a lump sum at closing. From day one, you make fixed monthly payments of principal plus interest for a set term, typically 5 to 20 years. The payment never changes. The rate never changes. When you make the last payment, the loan is gone.

A HELOC is a revolving credit line. Think of it as a very large, home-backed credit card. You're approved for a maximum amount (say, $60,000), but you don't take the money upfront. Instead, you have a draw period — usually 10 years — during which you can borrow, repay, and re-borrow up to your limit. During the draw period, your minimum payment is usually just the interest on what you've drawn — meaning the money you've borrowed stays borrowed. None of your monthly payment goes toward paying it back. Then the repayment period begins: typically 10 to 20 more years of payments that chip away at both the principal and the interest on whatever balance remains.

The rate on a HELOC is variable — it's tied to the U.S. Prime Rate (the benchmark most consumer loans track, currently 6.75%) plus a markup the lender adds, called the margin. When the Federal Reserve raises or cuts interest rates, the Prime Rate usually follows within weeks — which means your HELOC rate, and your monthly payment, change too.

Two things to notice. First, the gap between HELOC and home equity loan rates is narrow — roughly 1 percentage point — so rate is no longer the thing that settles the choice. Second, a HELOC's rate is a snapshot that can move; a home equity loan's rate is frozen at signing. If you think the Fed will cut further in 2026, the HELOC moves with you. If you think rates will hold or rise, the home equity loan locks you in.

The math, for a $60,000 need

Abstractions don't make decisions. Dollars do. Here's what the two products look like for a homeowner borrowing $60,000 at April 2026 average rates. We'll compare three scenarios: a standard home equity loan, a typical interest-only HELOC, and the disciplined version of a HELOC where the borrower voluntarily makes full principal-plus-interest payments from day one.

Scenario

$60,000 borrowed at April 2026 rates

Home equity loan — $60,000 at 8.05% fixed, 10 years

  • Monthly payment: $729 (same every month)
  • Total interest paid: $27,480
  • Total cost: $87,480

HELOC (the path most borrowers take) — $60,000 at 7.14%, interest only for the 10-year draw, then principal + interest for 10 more years

  • Draw period payment (years 1–10): $357/mo
  • Repayment period payment (years 11–20): $700/mo — payments nearly double
  • Total interest paid (if rate never changes): ~$66,840
  • Total cost: ~$126,840

HELOC (the disciplined version) — $60,000 at 7.14%, paying principal + interest from day one, 10-year term

  • Monthly payment: $701
  • Total interest paid: $24,120
  • Total cost: $84,120 — $3,360 less than the home equity loan

All figures assume the HELOC rate stays flat for the full term — a generous assumption. In reality, Prime can move at each Fed meeting.

Read those three outcomes carefully. The HELOC beats the home equity loan in total cost only if the borrower pays both principal and interest from day one. Take the interest-only option most borrowers take, and you save roughly $370/month during the 10-year draw period — real money back in your pocket each month. But that's not saving. It's delay. At year 10, you still owe the full $60,000 — and you'll spend the next decade paying it off, ending up $39,360 deeper in interest than if you'd taken the fixed home equity loan.

The HELOC doesn't save most borrowers money. It delays the bill — and the delay is expensive.

What happens if the HELOC rate moves

The table above assumes a flat rate for 20 years. That's not what happens in real life. This just happened: between January 2022 and August 2023, during the Fed's most recent tightening cycle, the Prime Rate went from 3.25% to 8.50% — adding more than five percentage points in 18 months. A HELOC taken in early 2022 more than doubled in cost before 2023 ended.

Here's the same $60,000 HELOC under three rate paths:

Under a 4-point rate rise, the HELOC ends up costing more than three times as much in total interest as the home equity loan. That's not a prediction — it's a stress test. You don't buy insurance because you expect a fire; you buy it because fires exist. The home equity loan is, in this sense, rate insurance.

Five things that surprise people

1. The payment shock at year 10 is not a rumor

When a HELOC transitions from interest-only draw to paying down both principal and interest, the monthly payment doesn't drift up gradually. It jumps, often by 50% to 100%, on a single billing cycle. On a $25,000 HELOC at 7.75%, the minimum payment more than doubles the month the draw period ends. This isn't a disclosure gotcha — the transition is spelled out in the closing paperwork. But the CFPB has logged more than a decade of complaints from borrowers who read the disclosure at closing, filed it away, and were blindsided when the jump actually arrived. The paperwork is not a plan. Put the year-10 date on your calendar now, and model the higher payment against your budget before you take the line — not after.

2. HELOCs charge fees for not borrowing

Annual maintenance fees of $25 to $250 are common, charged whether you use the line or not. Some lenders layer on inactivity fees of $200–$500 if you go a year without drawing — a penalty for not putting your home on the line. Home equity loans don't have either. Once the loan is closed, there's nothing to maintain and nothing to activate. If you're opening a HELOC as a “just in case” emergency cushion, treat the annual fee as an insurance premium and decide whether the insurance is worth the cost before you sign.

3. Many new HELOCs require a mandatory upfront draw

This is the biggest shift in the home equity market in years, and it's still flying under the radar. Nonbank HELOC lenders — Figure and newer fintech providers — now require borrowers to take 50% to 100% of their approved line as a mandatory draw at closing. Figure, currently the largest nonbank HELOC originator, requires a 100% draw at closing: the entire approved amount lands in your account whether you need it or not.

The math on that is painful. On a $120,000 Figure HELOC at 7.20%, a borrower who actually needed $30,000 for a kitchen renovation would end up paying interest on the full $120,000 — roughly $540/month extra on the $90,000 they never meant to spend. The whole reason to choose a HELOC — drawing what you need, when you need it — is gone. The rate risk is still there. Traditional banks and credit unions mostly still offer draw-as-you-go HELOCs. Ask plainly before applying, and read the “initial advance” line on the disclosure.

4. A HELOC can be frozen; a home equity loan cannot

Under federal consumer-protection rules, a HELOC lender can freeze or reduce your line if property values fall meaningfully, if you lose income or start missing payments on other debts, or under a handful of other conditions spelled out in the contract. During the 2008 crisis, that's exactly what happened. Countrywide froze an estimated 122,000 HELOCs; USAA froze or reduced some 15,000 more; Bank of America, Chase, and Citibank followed.

On a retirement-focused forum years later, one USAA customer put it bluntly: “If I had a balance, I could still pay it down. But I was not permitted to draw on it. They said they did it to everyone in my state, it wasn't personal — they just wanted to reduce their exposure.” A Minnesota homeowner had their line frozen despite never being late on payments and having only $13,000 drawn against a $55,000 ceiling. At least one Countrywide customer was frozen mid-renovation and left unable to pay the builder.

Once a home equity loan is funded, there's nothing to freeze. The money is already in your account. The freeze risk is not a theory. It's not history either — as of Q1 2026, national listing prices are down 2.2% year-over-year, with some metros seeing 7% declines. That's exactly the environment the rules are written to permit freezes in.

5. “No closing costs” usually means there's a leash

Many lenders advertise HELOCs with zero closing costs. Read the fine print. It's almost always paired with an early-termination fee that claws those waived costs back if you close the account within 24–36 months. Truist can claw back up to $10,000. Bank of America charges $450 within 36 months. U.S. Bank charges 1% of the original line, up to $500, within 30 months. These aren't scams — they make sense for the lender — but they need to be on your radar if there's any chance you'll refinance or pay off early.

Which product wins, and for whom

Strip away the marketing, and the decision reduces to a handful of tests.

Choose a HELOC when

  • Your expenses are genuinely phased — multi-year renovation, tuition over several years, ongoing medical bills.
  • You may not need the full approved amount, and paying interest only on what you draw saves real money.
  • You think rates will hold or fall, and you want the upside if they do.
  • You want a standby credit line for emergencies, and you're fine treating the annual fee as insurance.
  • You're disciplined enough to pay down principal during the draw period — the one case where the HELOC's lower rate actually pays off.

Choose a home equity loan when

  • You need the full amount now — a one-time expense like a roof, a major medical bill, or debt consolidation.
  • Payment certainty matters to you. Fixed income, tight budget, or simply a low tolerance for surprise.
  • You're consolidating high-interest debt and want the discipline of a fixed payoff schedule with no option to re-borrow.
  • Your local housing market is softening, and you'd rather not risk a frozen credit line.
  • You'll hold the loan 5+ years — the longer you carry it, the more the locked rate pays off.

When neither one is right

Both products put your home on the line. There are situations where the right answer is a third option — or no borrowing at all.

  • You'd drop below 15–20% equity after borrowing. Thin equity cushions are dangerous in a flat or falling market. You could end up underwater — owing more than the house is worth — and unable to sell without bringing cash to closing.
  • Your income is unstable. Both products turn your home into collateral. If there's a real chance of losing your income, a personal loan — unsecured, smaller — is usually safer even at a higher rate.
  • You're borrowing to spend, not to build. Vacations, cars, wedding expenses. Borrowing against your home for anything that doesn't build something lasting or fix a real money problem is how people end up with decades of compounding interest on a two-week trip.
  • The problem is spending, not rate. If high-rate credit card debt is the reason you're here, consolidating it into a HELOC or home equity loan lowers the interest but doesn't fix what caused the debt. Navy Federal's own consumer education page is blunt about this: consolidation “addresses the symptom but not the problem if overspending caused the debt in the first place.” A well-known pattern on personal-finance forums: the cards get paid off, run back up within 18 months, and now the borrower carries the card debt again and the home-secured debt. The home equity loan at least has structure — fixed amount, no re-borrow. The HELOC doesn't.
  • You can't absorb a 3–4 point rate rise. Run the stress test. If it breaks the payment, the HELOC is too risky. And if the home equity loan's fixed payment is already a stretch, you're borrowing too much.

The decision, in one paragraph

If you know exactly what you need, need it all now, and want a payment you can circle on the calendar for the next decade — get the home equity loan. If your expenses are truly spread out over years, you may not use the full line, you're disciplined about paying down what you draw, and you're borrowing from a bank or credit union that doesn't require a mandatory upfront draw — get the HELOC, and pay down principal on your own from day one. If neither fits cleanly, run the specific numbers. The gap between products is narrow enough that small differences in your situation swing the answer — and the stakes are too big to hand the decision to your lender's sales script.
