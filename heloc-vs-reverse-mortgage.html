HELOC vs. reverse mortgage:
which one actually fits a retiree's life?

One product removes the monthly payment. One preserves the equity. The right answer depends on your income stability, how long you plan to stay, and what you want to leave behind.

The short answer

A HELOC is almost always cheaper than a reverse mortgage over time — but only if you can actually afford the monthly payment from your retirement income, including if the variable rate rises. A reverse mortgage (HECM) removes the monthly payment entirely and locks in the right to stay in the home — at the cost of roughly 2–4% of your home's value in upfront costs and a loan balance that grows every month until you move, sell, or pass. For retirees with stable income and adult heirs, the HELOC usually wins. For retirees whose cash flow genuinely can't carry a monthly payment, the reverse mortgage is the one that prevents a worse outcome. The right answer depends less on the product and more on your honest cash-flow picture.

For most of the financial-products landscape, "better" and "cheaper" are close cousins. Reverse mortgages break the pattern. A HECM — the FHA-insured reverse mortgage, which is nearly all of them — carries upfront costs measured in tens of thousands of dollars, a loan balance that compounds for the rest of your life in the home, and a long enough history of regulatory action that the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has started flagging its marketing as potentially predatory. On pure cost per dollar borrowed, a HELOC wins almost every time. And yet the reverse mortgage is sometimes, genuinely, the correct answer — because the HELOC requires a monthly payment, and for some retirees, making that payment from a fixed income is the actual risk.

This is not a decision that rewards the kind of breezy pros-and-cons comparison most sites offer. The reader here has specific circumstances: a retirement-age home, a retirement-age income, adult children who may or may not be counting on an inheritance, and often a surviving spouse whose age, name on the title, and future income matter. Below: what each product actually is, the math over a realistic horizon, the risks the marketing leaves out, and the honest decision framework for when each one is the right tool.

The fundamental tradeoff

At the core, the choice is this: a HELOC lets you borrow against equity at a lower rate, but requires you to make a monthly payment from whatever retirement income you have. A reverse mortgage eliminates the monthly payment — and locks in the right to stay in the home until you move, sell, or die — but charges for that peace in two ways. First, the upfront costs are substantial: the Federal Housing Administration's initial mortgage insurance premium alone (the FHA insures these loans to protect both borrowers and lenders) is 2% of the home's value, plus origination, title, and HUD counseling fees. On a $500,000 home, that's roughly $18,000 in closing costs, almost always rolled into the loan balance. Second, the loan balance grows every month instead of shrinking, because you're not paying the interest — it's just added on top.

Over time, the growing balance eats equity that would otherwise pass to your heirs. On a $100,000 initial HECM draw at today's 7.68% fixed rate plus the 0.5% ongoing insurance premium, the loan balance roughly doubles in ten years — to about $249,000. A HELOC with the same $100,000 balance, held at interest-only, still owes $100,000 at year ten. The difference, roughly $150,000, is the cost of not having made any monthly payments.

That framing is the right one. The reverse mortgage isn't cheaper. It's the product for borrowers whose cash flow genuinely can't support a monthly payment — where the alternative isn't a HELOC that's $150,000 cheaper over ten years, but a HELOC that triggers default inside of two.

The math, over 10 years

Two retirees face the same need: $100,000 of home equity tapped over ten years. Both have $500,000 homes with no existing mortgage. They differ on cash flow — and on whether a monthly payment actually fits.

Scenario

$100,000 tapped over 10 years, $500,000 home paid off

The HELOC path — $100,000 line at 7.02% variable

  • Upfront cost: ~$1,000
  • Monthly interest-only payment: ~$585/mo (for 10 years of draw period)
  • Total interest paid over 10 years: ~$70,200
  • Loan balance at year 10: $100,000 (no principal paid)
  • Equity remaining (on unchanged $500K home): $400,000
  • Rate risk: if Prime rises, so does the $585 payment

The reverse mortgage path — $100,000 HECM draw at 7.68% fixed

  • Upfront cost (rolled into balance): ~$18,000 (2% initial MIP + origination + title + counseling)
  • Monthly payment: $0
  • Starting balance after closing: ~$118,000
  • Loan balance at year 10 (accrued interest + 0.5% annual MIP): ~$249,000
  • Equity remaining (on unchanged $500K home): ~$251,000
  • Cash-flow risk: none from the loan itself; but property taxes and insurance still apply, and default triggers on those

Both scenarios assume flat home values. Moderate appreciation (3%/yr) moves the absolute equity numbers up but leaves the ~$150K gap between paths roughly intact in dollar terms.

Read the rows side by side. The reverse mortgage costs roughly $150,000 more in lost equity over ten years on the same amount borrowed. That gap is the price of "no monthly payment." For a retiree with $8,500 a month of income and a $585 HELOC payment that's comfortably affordable, paying $150,000 of future heirs' equity to avoid $585 a month is a bad trade. For a retiree whose entire income is $3,200 a month of Social Security and whose property taxes alone are $400, the $585 HELOC payment is the trigger that would cost them the house to begin with. The same $150,000 "cost" now prevents the worse outcome.

The reverse mortgage isn't cheaper. It's the product that prevents a default a HELOC would have caused.

The real risk most marketing omits

The common complaint about reverse mortgages — "they take your house after you die" — is mostly not how the actual losses happen. The loan comes due at death or move-out, and the heirs usually sell the home to pay it off; if there's equity left, they keep it. That's working as designed.

The actual risk is different and more common. A HECM borrower remains responsible for property taxes, homeowner's insurance, and ongoing maintenance. If any of those lapse, the lender can declare default and foreclose — even though no loan payment has been missed. A USA TODAY analysis of 1.3 million HECM loan records found that nearly 100,000 ended in foreclosure, roughly one in thirteen, and the overwhelming majority of those came from property-charge defaults, not mortgage-payment defaults. A retiree who took the reverse mortgage specifically to stabilize cash flow can still lose the home if property taxes rise faster than their budget can absorb, or an insurance premium spikes after a storm season.

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has been active on servicing problems in the product too. In June 2024, the CFPB ordered Sutherland Global Services and NOVAD Management Consulting to pay $11.5 million in consumer redress and a $5 million penalty for reverse-mortgage servicing failures — including failing to respond to borrower inquiries and failing to process requests for payment assistance from borrowers who had fallen behind on property charges. NOVAD received a permanent ban from reverse mortgage servicing. On the marketing side, CFPB data shows reverse mortgage direct-mail advertising has increased fourfold since 2019, targeted heavily at older homeowners with high equity and lower incomes. The regulator has flagged the targeting as a concern.

None of this makes the product wrong. It does mean the borrower needs to plan for property-charge costs that will rise — and may need to set aside part of any reverse-mortgage proceeds explicitly to cover those future obligations, rather than spending all of it on current cash flow.

Four things borrowers get wrong

1. The loan balance doubles faster than you'd guess

At a 7.68% fixed rate plus the 0.5% ongoing insurance premium, a reverse-mortgage balance roughly doubles every ten years. A $100,000 initial draw at age 65 can grow to $400,000 by age 85 — not because anything went wrong, but because unpaid interest compounds. Borrowers who take a HECM in their sixties and live into their nineties often end with a loan balance larger than their home's appreciated value. The FHA-required insurance protects you from owing more than the home is worth (no personal liability for the shortfall). It does not leave anything for your heirs.

2. The HUD counseling session is a real decision gate — treat it that way

Every HECM borrower must complete HUD-approved counseling before closing. It's a 60–90 minute session, it costs $125–$200, and it exists specifically to catch exploitation, explain alternatives (including HELOCs, downsizing, and just not borrowing), and ensure you understand what you're signing. Treat it as a genuine evaluation, not a formality. If any part of the session feels rushed, or you leave it unclear on any element of the loan, that is the signal to pause — not proceed.

3. A non-borrowing spouse under 62 is a specific risk

If you're 70 and your spouse is 60 and you put only yourself on a HECM, HUD's non-borrowing spouse protections may let your spouse stay in the home after your death — but only if they meet ongoing requirements (maintain the home, pay taxes and insurance, remain married to you throughout your lifetime). They cannot access any remaining HECM funds. If they remarry or leave the home for more than 12 months, the loan becomes due. A HELOC doesn't have this problem because it doesn't condition itself on who's still living in the home.

4. Small reverse mortgages don't earn back their upfront costs

The 2% initial MIP alone is $10,000 on a $500,000 home. Add origination, title, and counseling, and you're at $15,000–$20,000 before you see the first dollar of cash. If you only need $40,000 or $50,000, the upfront costs are 30–50% of the funds you'll actually receive. For small amounts, a HELOC (if you can afford the payment) or a home equity loan is almost always cheaper — and often a better fit than the HECM's standby line-of-credit feature for retirees who don't need the full $100K+ range.

Which product wins, and for whom

The decision depends less on the product and more on your cash flow, your timeline, and your heirs.

Choose a HELOC when

  • Your retirement income comfortably supports the monthly payment — even if HELOC rates rise another 2–3 percentage points from here.
  • You plan to sell or move within 5–10 years, so reverse-mortgage upfront costs won't amortize.
  • You have heirs and preserving equity matters to you.
  • Your spouse is under 62 and you want to avoid the complications that come with leaving a spouse off a reverse-mortgage loan.
  • Your need is a one-time project (a home repair, a medical event, a bridge between homes), not ongoing income.

Choose a reverse mortgage when

  • Your cash flow genuinely can't carry a HELOC's monthly payment without risk of default.
  • You intend to age in place — meaning you'll likely stay in the home until death.
  • You're 75 or older, where the age-scaled borrowing limit is higher and the upfront costs earn themselves back over a longer expected horizon.
  • You need a standby line of credit that grows unused. (The HECM line-of-credit option has this unusual feature: unused funds accrue at the same rate as the loan itself, so your available credit actually increases over time.)
  • Your heirs are not counting on a substantial inheritance from the home, or you've had an explicit conversation about it.

When neither is right

Some circumstances make home-equity borrowing the wrong tool entirely.

  • Downsizing would solve the same problem without any loan. Selling a $500,000 home and buying a $300,000 one frees $200,000 in cash, eliminates the higher property taxes and maintenance, and leaves more for heirs than either product. For many retirees who are considering a reverse mortgage primarily for cash flow, downsizing is the better answer and rarely gets enough serious weight.
  • There's any risk of not being able to pay property taxes or insurance going forward. Both products default when property charges lapse. For a reverse mortgage, this is the most common path to foreclosure. If the underlying budget doesn't work, neither loan saves it.
  • A family member is willing to help. Family cash-flow help, when it exists, is almost always cheaper than either product. The emotional complexity is real, but so is $150,000 of equity.
  • Cognitive decline is a concern. Both products are complex; reverse mortgages especially. Any indication that the borrower's ability to manage financial decisions is declining should trigger a family conversation and a delay, not a closing date. The HUD counseling session is designed to catch this. It should be a real evaluation, with the non-borrowing spouse and any involved adult children present if possible.
  • You could use a Medicare-supported or state-supported program instead. If the cash-flow problem is medical, long-term-care, or utility-related, federal and state programs — LIHEAP (the federal energy-assistance program), Medicaid, and property-tax deferral programs for seniors that most states offer — may solve it without any home-equity borrowing at all. A HUD-approved housing counselor can help identify options.

The decision, in one paragraph

A HELOC is cheaper almost every time the cash-flow math works. A reverse mortgage is the product that prevents a default a HELOC would have triggered — by removing the monthly payment entirely, at the cost of roughly 2–4% of your home's value up front and a loan balance that grows every month until you leave the home. For retirees with stable income, adult heirs, and plans to move within ten years, the HELOC almost always wins. For retirees on truly tight cash flow who intend to age in place and whose heirs aren't counting on a substantial equity inheritance, the reverse mortgage is the honest answer. The worst version of the decision is the one made from a sales pitch. The best version is the one made after a HUD counseling session, an open conversation with your family, and a careful look at your next ten years of property-tax bills. The right product is the one that fits the life you actually have.
